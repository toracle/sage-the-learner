= Episode 12: The Moment Intuition Strikes Like Lightning

[quote]
____
"Intuition is not a gift from the gods. It is the countless experiences accumulated within you, bursting out in a single instant."
____

'''

It was one afternoon as Sage walked along a forest trail.

Far ahead, a young deer was drinking from a small stream. At that moment, a shrub at the forest's edge rustled. A low rumble was heard, and a large mountain cat with tawny fur began lowering its body toward the deer.

Something "switched on" in Sage's mind.

_There was no time to analyze._ The mountain cat was gathering power in its hind legs, and the deer still had its head bent down drinking. Before Sage had even formed the thought "what should I do," his body was already moving.

He cried out loudly and spread both arms, stepping toward the mountain cat. At the same time, he stomped on a dry branch. Crack. Crack. Crack.

The deer startled and bolted in the opposite direction, and the mountain cat had its attention pulled for a moment by this unexpected intruder. In that interval Sage stepped back and hid himself behind a large tree. The mountain cat looked around for a moment, then, realizing its hunting opportunity was lost, slipped away quietly into the forest.

Sage pressed his back against the tree and slowly steadied his breath. His heart was beating fast.

_What decision did I just make?_

He had no sense of having made a "decision." He simply moved.

'''

That evening, he told the Sage the story.

"Sage, something strange happened. I had no thought at all, yet my body moved first. I did not even know whether what I had done was right — and yet the deer survived and so did I. Was it simply good luck?"

The Sage set down his teacup and smiled.

"It was too precise to call luck. You noticed from the mountain cat's posture that it was 'about to spring.' You knew you needed to disperse its attention with sound and movement. You knew not to step between the deer and the predator…. All of that judgment happened in an instant."

"But I have no memory of 'thinking' any of that."

"That is exactly the point," the Sage said. "All the many hours you have spent in the forest until now, the experiences of observing the movements of animals, the memories of surviving dangerous moments… all of that had been engraved in you as 'patterns.' Intuition is the burst of those patterns in an instant — it did not spring from empty air."

Sage nodded quietly.

"Then," he asked, "is there no intuition for someone with no experience?"

The Sage answered: "It is only the 'depth' of intuition that differs. But without accumulated experience, there is no flash of insight either."

A moment of silence passed. The Sage refilled his teacup and opened his mouth slowly.

"But Sage, I now want to know more specifically where your intuition came from. May I ask you a few things?"

Sage nodded.

"When you saw the shrub move, you didn't simply pass it off as 'the wind' — you sensed danger. In that very moment, what exactly caught your eye?"

Sage closed his eyes for a moment. "…The direction of the shaking was wrong. The wind shakes from the top, but that day the lower stems were shaking first. It was the way something heavy was moving inside."

"And?"

"There was a sound. A low, short rumble — I knew it was not the sound of 'searching' but the sound of 'focusing.' The rumble of a predator that has sighted prey and is concentrating is different. More constant, not cut off in the middle."

The Sage smiled gently. "Go on."

"When I saw the mountain cat's hindquarters…" Sage continued slowly. "Its center of gravity had shifted backward. That is the posture for 'just before springing.' The front paws spread slightly and the tail drops low, maintaining horizontal. At that moment, less than five seconds remained."

The Sage asked quietly: "Where did you learn this?"

Sage paused. "I'm not sure I even 'learned' it… I had read a great deal of material about natural history in the valley. There were records about the behavior patterns of hunting animals. And on the way to this mountain, I spent several months in the forest and directly observed predators from a distance on several occasions."

"One more thing," the Sage added. "You did not leap between the predator and the deer. You disrupted it without making yourself the target. That judgment was?"

Sage thought for a moment. "…Predators respond to things that move. If I had suddenly rushed toward the deer, I would have looked like prey myself. So I needed only to 'break the mountain cat's focus.' Toward the mountain cat, not toward the deer — but not too close."

The Sage watched Sage for a long time before speaking.

"Can you see it now. You had what you had read in books, what you had learned in the forest through your body, and the memories of surviving crises. It was simply that they were stored not in the form of 'knowledge' but in the form that activates the moment you perceive the situation. That is intuition."

Sage looked down at his hands. For the first time he truly felt that these hands were connected to all those old natural history texts from years ago, and to every one of those mornings he had spent in the forest at dawn.

"Sage," he asked quietly. "Then for someone who knows nothing of the forest… how can this intuition be passed on?"

The Sage answered: "By doing exactly what we just did."

'''

== Where Does Intuition Come From: Judgment at the Moment Experience Is Condensed

=== The Nature of Intuition — Not Mystery, But Compressed Experience

Many people think of intuition as "a sense you're born with" or "inspiration." But the results of decades of research by cognitive scientist *Gary Klein* studying first responders, doctors, and military commanders were different.

Intuition is *what is activated automatically the moment mental representations formed through experience perceive the situation*.

He systematized this as *Naturalistic Decision Making (NDM)* theory, presenting the *Recognition-Primed Decision (RPD)* model as the core mechanism.

'''

=== The RPD Model: Experts Recognize Rather Than Compare

Typically we imagine decision-making like this:

[quote]
____
List several options → Compare the pros and cons of each → Choose the best one.
____

But the field experts Klein observed were different. They *didn't compare — they simulated in their mind the first action that came to them the moment they "recognized what kind of situation this is,"* and if that seemed like "it would probably work well enough," they immediately acted on it.

Not comparing, but *recognizing a pattern and verifying it through simulation*.

[source]
----
[Situation recognition]
    ↓
Pattern matching: "This is an X situation"
    ↓
First action comes to mind
    ↓
Mental simulation: "What would happen if I did this?"
    ↓
OK → Execute / Not OK → Move to next action
----

What Sage did before the mountain cat was exactly this. The pattern "mountain cat in springing posture → distract with noise → gap created for deer to escape" was activated unconsciously.

'''

=== Drawing Out Expert Tacit Knowledge — Surfacing and Transferring Intuition

The NDM research did not begin from simple academic curiosity. There was a practical problem in the field.

Experienced firefighters, neonatal intensive care nurses, air force pilots possessed judgment that new hires struggled to match even after years of learning. Yet no one could explain exactly what that judgment was or how it had been built. Even the experts themselves could only say "I know by feeling."

The researchers' goal was clear: *Identify the nature of that "feeling" and make it possible for new hires to rapidly acquire the core patterns without waiting ten years.*

To do that, they first had to draw out what was inside the expert's mind.

'''

*What Experts See Differently: Cues and Strategies*

Recall the questions the Sage asked Sage.

* "When the shrub moved, what exactly caught your eye?"
* "How was that sound different from other sounds?"
* "Why did you move toward the mountain cat rather than toward the deer?"

These questions were designed to draw out what *cues* Sage had responded to and how he had connected those cues to *strategies*. Cues are "signals visible only to the expert's eyes," and strategies are "principles of action that operate when that signal is received."

In Sage's case, what was surfaced was:

[cols="1,1", options="header"]
|===
| Cue | Strategy
| Shrub shaking from the bottom up | Heavy living creature inside → heighten vigilance
| Low, steady rumble, unbroken | State of focus, not searching → attack imminent
| Hindquarters retreating, tail dropping horizontally | Spring within 5 seconds → immediate intervention needed
| Direction of the predator's gaze | Determine angle of intervention — must not come between deer and me
|===

Sage had not processed these cues consciously. But when traced back through the Sage's questions, they could all be put into language. Tacit knowledge had been converted into explicit knowledge.

'''

*Why This Matters: The Transferability of Intuition*

Cues and strategies surfaced this way now become things that can be taught. In nursing research, investigators extracted through interviews and observation what cues experienced nurses used to detect signs of infection in newborns before others could. These included subtle changes in skin tone, slight irregularities in feeding patterns, and changes in the tone of crying. When these were organized and applied to education for new nurses, the time to proficiency was meaningfully shortened.

The same was true in firefighter training. What signals veteran firefighters were reading when they felt "this building is about to collapse" was systematically extracted and embedded in scenario-based training.

Expert intuition was not a mysterious talent — it was *structured experience that could be surfaced and transferred*.

'''

=== Developing Intuition — Turning Experience Into Patterns

Intuition depends not simply on the quantity of experience but on *the quality of experience accompanied by reflective practice*. Intuition does not need to be passively accumulated. *Intuition can be excavated and trained.*

'''

*① Repeated exposure + Immediate feedback* +
Encounter the same type of situation many times, but each time verify "was my judgment right?" Repetition without feedback can solidify wrong patterns. Experience without a feedback loop is not practice — it is merely the passage of time.

'''

*② Excavate your own intuition into language*

Most expert intuition is *tacit knowledge*. Even the experts themselves often say "I just felt that way," unable to explain exactly what they saw and judged.

The most powerful way to draw this out is *an interview that traces back decisive moments*. Ask yourself, or ask together with a colleague:

* "What cues made me sense danger at that moment?"
* "What felt different from usual?"
* "Why didn't I think of other options besides that action?"
* "If I were a novice, what would I have missed in this situation?"

When Sage told the story to the Sage after the mountain cat incident and traced back "what cues caused me to act that way," that was exactly this process. Not letting experiences flow past, but pulling to the surface in language the patterns hidden within them.

'''

*③ Scenario training and borrowing from masters*

If experience is still shallow in a domain, two approaches in parallel are effective. One is *scenario training close to reality*. When reading a case study, stop at a specific point and directly predict "what would I have judged here?" then compare with what actually happened. Simply reading is not enough for patterns to become engraved.

The other is *drawing out an experienced person's intuition through interview*. Just as the Sage did with Sage, when you ask "what exactly caught your eye at that moment?" patterns and strategies the person themselves did not know they had become articulated. This is also why apprenticeship-based learning has remained effective for so long. The master's pattern recognition is transferred through questions and observation.

'''

____
Intuition is the language of compressed experience. To want intuition without experience is like expecting a harvest without seeds.
____
