= Epilogue: Those Who Draw New Maps

"A teacher's greatest success is when students no longer need the teacher."

== The Change in the Valley of Stagnation

A year had passed since Sage returned to the village.

At first, no one paid any attention to his teaching. The elders still carried thick books around, and the young scholars were absorbed in memorization contests. When Sage said he would teach "the way of learning," someone laughed. "Does knowing the way put food on the table? You need to know the answers."

But Sage was not shaken.

He placed a few small wooden chairs in one corner of the square and sat down. Every morning, he said, anyone with a question was welcome. At first, children came. Children did not stand on ceremony the way adults did. They asked Sage about the river, about the stars, about why leaves fall in winter.

Sage did not give them answers. Instead, he thought together with them.

"What do you know right now and what don't you know?"

"Where have you seen something like this before?"

"Suppose you're wrong. What explanation would come out of that?"

The children were puzzled at first. There had never been an adult who asked questions like this. But soon they caught on. After thinking with Sage, they discovered they could think in similar ways on their own.

'''

== The River Spreads

Change came slowly but surely.

One of the children who had learned to ask questions at Sage's square started asking his younger sibling in the same way at home. That child's mother came to find Sage one day and said: "My child has changed. Instead of giving up when he doesn't know something, he tries to find something similar."

One young scholar had initially mocked Sage, but after a crushing failure in an examination quietly appeared in the square. "Why can't I remember what I memorized?" That was his first real question.

Sage told him the story of the map.

"When you try to memorize everything all at once without landmarks, your mind becomes foggy like it is covered in mist."

The scholar sat in silence for a long while and then said: "I have been trying to memorize the whole forest at once all this time."

'''

== The Sage's Letter

One autumn, a traveler who had come down from the mountain delivered a letter to Sage.

It was in the Sage's handwriting.

'''

_To Sage._

_After you returned to the Valley of Stagnation, I walked the forest every morning. The fork where you first lost your way, the hill where you found the landmarks, the ravine beyond where you and Dowon combined your two maps……_

_Those places look a little different now. Not because your footprints remain. But because I remember that you learned at those places._

_What I taught you was not, in truth, very much. I was only beside you so that what was already inside you could open its eyes. True learning always happens within the person who is learning._

_I heard carried on the wind the news that you are now becoming such a place for others._

_Keep doing that. Do not give answers — plant questions. Do not give maps — teach the way to draw maps._

_That is how learning flows like a river._

_— From the Mountain of Contemplation, the Sage_

'''

Sage looked at the letter for a long time.

At that moment, voices of children came from the square. The sound of them asking each other questions, answering each other, laughing even when wrong and thinking again.

Sage folded the letter and tucked it into his chest.

And walked toward the square.

'''

== Your Story

Sage's journey has ended. But the questions that journey opened have not ended.

The way of learning does not discriminate by era. The principles Sage learned in the forest — finding the landmarks first, connecting with meaning, reflecting from failure, building bridges with metaphor, combining with another person's map — all operate at a desk, in a meeting room, and in conversation with a child.

If you called to mind a scene while reading this book, that is transfer.

If you felt "I had a time like that too," that is analogy.

If even after you close this book one question lingers in your heart, that is a sign that learning is alive.
